# Transmission Medium

A transmission medium is the physical or wireless path through which data travels from sender to receiver.

---

## Types of Transmission Media

### Guided (Wired)
- Twisted Pair Cable
- Coaxial Cable
- Fibre Optic Cable

### Unguided (Wireless)
- Radio Waves
- Microwaves
- Infrared

**Note :** Guided media generally provide higher speed and reliability than unguided media.

### Description
Twisted pair cables consist of pairs of copper wires twisted together to reduce electromagnetic interference (EMI).

- Normally contains 8 copper wires
- Arranged into 4 twisted pairs
- Twisting helps reduce noise, not completely eliminate it

### Types

- **UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair)** – most commonly used
- **STP (Shielded Twisted Pair)**

### UTP Cable Categories (Copper Cable)

- UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair) cables are copper cables commonly used for Ethernet and LAN networking.  
- Different categories (CAT) support different speeds and applications.
- UTP cable categories are classified primarily based on the data rate (speed) they support.

<img width="450" height="450" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/6fc36b4f-92d7-4687-9d98-cb71e0eb8c7b" />


### What Does CAT Mean ?
- CAT refers to the category of the cable
- Higher CAT number = higher speed + better performance

### CAT1
- Speed: Up to 1 Mbps
- Used for: Old telephone lines
- Not used for networking today

### CAT2
- Speed: Up to 4 Mbps
- Used in: Old Token Ring networks
- Obsolete today

### CAT3
- Speed: Up to 10 Mbps
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used for:
  - Token Ring
  - Early Ethernet (10BASE-T)
- Rarely used now

### CAT4
- Speed: Up to 16 Mbps
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used in: Token Ring networks
- Obsolete

### CAT5
- Speed: Up to 100 Mbps
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used for:
  - Ethernet
  - Fast Ethernet
- Mostly replaced by CAT5e

### CAT5e (Enhanced CAT5)
- Speed: Up to 1 Gbps (1000 Mbps)
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used for:
  - Ethernet
  - Gigabit Ethernet
- Most commonly used in homes and offices

### CAT6
- Speed: Up to 10 Gbps
- Max Length:
  - 100 meters (1 Gbps)
  - 55 meters (10 Gbps)
- Used for:
  - Gigabit Ethernet
  - 10G Ethernet (short distances)
- Better shielding than CAT5e

### CAT6a (Augmented CAT6)
- Speed: Up to 10 Gbps
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used for:
  - High-speed networks
  - Data centers
- Improved performance over CAT6

### CAT7
- Speed: Up to 10 Gbps
- Max Length: 100 meters
- Used for:
  - High-performance networks
- Better shielding and less interference

### Quick Summary Table

| Category | Max Speed | Max Length | Common Use |
|--------|----------|------------|-----------|
| CAT5e | 1 Gbps | 100 m | Home / Office LAN |
| CAT6 | 10 Gbps | 55 m | High-speed LAN |
| CAT6a | 10 Gbps | 100 m | Data centers |
| CAT7 | 10 Gbps | 100 m | Enterprise networks |

### Cybersecurity Relevance
- Higher CAT cables reduce:
  - Signal loss
  - Interference
- Better cable quality improves:
  - Network reliability
  - Secure data transmission
- Physical access to cables can still be a security risk

> CAT5e is enough for most users, CAT6+ is used for high-speed and enterprise networks

### Advantages
- Cheap
- Easy to install and work with

### Disadvantages
- Low data rate
- Short range
- Low bandwidth
- Susceptible to interference

## Coaxial Cable

<img width="350" height="150" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/bba204c7-aedd-4b3a-99a0-2d029698f54e" />

### Description
A coaxial cable is a copper cable with:
- A central conductor
- Insulating layer
- Metallic shield for interference protection

Primarily used by cable TV companies to connect satellite systems to homes and businesses.

### Characteristics
- Better shielding than twisted pair
- Less interference

### Advantages
- Used in high-frequency applications
- Improved attenuation and shielding
- Less susceptible to noise than twisted pair
- High bandwidth
- Lower cable cost

### Disadvantages
- Bulky, thick, and stiff
- Expensive to install
- Security concerns
- Requires grounding

## Fibre Optic Cable

<img width="350" height="350" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/0e902152-8f98-4481-90dd-9314224d1a69" />

### Description
Fibre optic cables transmit data as light signals and can carry data over hundreds of miles at very high speeds.

Widely used for long-distance communication, including undersea cables connecting continents.

### Types of Fibre Optic Cables

### 1. Single Mode Fibre (SMF) – Step Index

Single Mode Fibre (SMF) uses a very narrow core, allowing only one light path (mode) to travel through the fibre.

#### Index Type : Step Index
- The refractive index changes suddenly (step-wise) between the core and cladding
- Light travels in a straight path with minimal reflection

#### Characteristics
- Very long transmission distance
- Extremely low signal loss (attenuation)
- Minimal dispersion

#### Usage
- Long-distance communication
- Undersea cables
- ISP backbone networks

### 2. Multi Mode Fibre (MMF) – Graded Index

Multi Mode Fibre (MMF) has a wider core, allowing multiple light paths (modes) to travel simultaneously.

#### Index Type :  Graded Index
- The refractive index gradually decreases from the center to the edge
- Light rays bend smoothly instead of bouncing sharply
- Reduces signal distortion compared to step index MMF

#### Characteristics
- Shorter distance compared to SMF
- Higher dispersion than SMF
- Lower cost than SMF

#### Usage
- Local Area Networks (LANs)
- Data centers
- Short-distance communication

## Comparison : SMF vs MMF

| Feature | Single Mode Fibre (SMF) | Multi Mode Fibre (MMF) |
|------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Core Size | Very small | Larger |
| Light Paths | Single | Multiple |
| Index Type | Step Index | Graded Index |
| Distance | Very long | Short |
| Cost | High | Lower |
| Common Use | Backbone, undersea | LANs, data centers |

### Characteristics
- Most immune to EMI
- Extremely high throughput
- Long-distance communication

### Advantages
- Extremely high bandwidth
- Thin and lightweight
- Resistant to EMI
- Longer transmission distance
- Low security risk

### Disadvantages
- High cost
- Very fragile
- Difficult to install
- Attenuation and dispersion issues

## Comparison of Twisted Pair, Coaxial, and Optical Fiber Cables

| Feature | Twisted Pair Cable | Coaxial Cable | Optical Fiber Cable |
|------|------------------|--------------|--------------------|
| Transmission Type | Electrical signals | Electrical signals | Light signals |
| Material | Copper wire pairs | Copper core with shielding | Glass or plastic fiber |
| Speed / Data Rate | Low to high (category-based) | High | Extremely high |
| Bandwidth | Low to moderate | High | Very high |
| Distance | Short (up to 100 m) | Medium | Very long (hundreds of km) |
| EMI Resistance | Low | Better due to shielding | Completely immune |
| Cable Size | Thin and flexible | Thick and stiff | Very thin and lightweight |
| Cost | Cheap | Moderate | Expensive |
| Installation | Easy | Difficult | Very difficult |
| Security | Easy to tap | Harder to tap | Very difficult to tap |
| Common Usage | LAN, Ethernet | Cable TV, broadband | Internet backbone, undersea cables |

## Cybersecurity Relevance
- **Twisted Pair**: High risk of packet sniffing and EMI-based attacks  
- **Coaxial**: Better shielding but still vulnerable to physical tapping  
- **Optical Fiber**: Most secure medium, difficult to tap and immune to EMI

# Cybersecurity Relevance of Transmission Media

## Twisted Pair & Coaxial Cables
- Vulnerable to wire tapping
- EMI leakage can lead to signal interception
- Physical access = high attack risk

## Fibre Optic Cable
- Very difficult to tap without detection
- Minimal signal leakage
- Preferred for secure communications
- Used in critical infrastructure and backbone networks

## Unguided Media (Wireless)
- Highly vulnerable to :
  - Eavesdropping
  - Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) attacks
  - Signal jamming
- Requires strong encryption and authentication

---

## Why Transmission Media Matter in Cybersecurity
Understanding transmission media helps in :
- Identifying physical attack vectors
- Designing secure networks
- Choosing appropriate cabling for sensitive data
- Preventing data interception
- Reducing signal leakage risks
